2 · Two kinds of variable (this choice drives the rest)

Pick the wrong summary for the variable type and the whole table is wrong. There are really just two families.

⌨️ The Stata command (you'll run this in the hands-on)
codebook, compact
📋 Result — computed live from the 200 patients
Variable                 Type         Summarise with        Picture with      
-----------------------  -----------  --------------------  ------------------
age, sbp, bmi, crp, los  Continuous   mean/median, SD/IQR   histogram, boxplot
sex, ward, diabetes      Categorical  counts & percentages  bar chart
💡 What this means
Continuous variables are measured on a scale where the gaps mean something (61 mmHg really is 2 more than 59). You summarise them with a centre and a spread. Categorical variables are just labels — averaging "ward" is meaningless — so you summarise them with counts and percentages.
🚗 In the hands-on (Stata's auto data)
In auto: mpg, price, weight are continuous; foreign (domestic/foreign) and rep78 are categorical.
🗣️ Say this
First question for every column: can I sensibly average it? Yes → continuous. No → categorical. The answer picks your summary and your plot.

3 · The centre — mean, median, mode

Three ways to answer "what's a typical patient?" — and the gap between them is itself information.

⌨️ The Stata command (you'll run this in the hands-on)
summarize age, detail
📋 Result — computed live from the 200 patients
age                
------------  -----
Obs             200
Mean          59.24
Std. dev.     14.69
Min              18
25%              51
Median (50%)     59
75%              70
Max              95
Skewness      -0.09
💡 What this means
For age the mean (59.2) and median (59) sit almost on top of each other. That near-match is the signature of a symmetric distribution — for age, either number is a fair 'typical patient'.
🗣️ Say this
Mean and median agree here, so age is symmetric — quote whichever you like.
⌨️ The Stata command (you'll run this in the hands-on)
summarize crp, detail
📋 Result — computed live from the 200 patients
crp                
------------  -----
Obs             200
Mean           6.98
Std. dev.      9.87
Min             0.3
25%             2.3
Median (50%)    4.0
75%             7.8
Max           100.6
Skewness       5.43
💡 What this means
Now look at CRP: the mean (7.0) is far above the median (4.0). A handful of very sick patients with huge CRP drag the mean upward, but the median is unmoved — half the patients are still below 4.0. When mean ≫ median, the data are right-skewed and the median is the honest centre.
🚗 In the hands-on (Stata's auto data)
Car price behaves exactly like CRP — a few expensive cars pull the mean above the median. Try summarize price, detail.
🗣️ Say this
Mean way above median = skew. One septic patient can't move the median, but it drags the mean — so for CRP, length-of-stay, cost, we report the median.
⚠️ Watch out
The mode (most common value) is only useful for categories or counts. For continuous measurements it's rarely worth reporting.

4 · The spread — range, IQR, variance, SD

Two wards can share the same mean BP yet be utterly different. Spread is the other half of the story.

⌨️ The Stata command (you'll run this in the hands-on)
tabstat age, stats(min p25 median p75 max range iqr sd variance)
📋 Result — computed live from the 200 patients
Statistic  Value  Plain meaning                              
---------  -----  -------------------------------------------
Range         77  max − min (sensitive to one extreme)       
IQR           19  middle 50% width (robust)                  
Variance   215.7  average squared distance from mean         
SD          14.7  typical distance from the mean (same units)
💡 What this means
SD is the one to internalise: it's the typical distance of a patient from the mean, in the original units (years here). With roughly-Normal data, about 95% of patients fall within mean ± 2 SD — here that's 29.9 to 88.6 years. That is exactly how clinical reference ranges are built. IQR (the middle-50% width) is the spread you quote alongside a median for skewed data.
🚗 In the hands-on (Stata's auto data)
tabstat mpg, stats(sd iqr range) on the auto data.
🗣️ Say this
SD is the typical gap from the mean. Mean ± 2 SD captures about 95% of people — that's where lab reference ranges come from.

5 · The descriptive table — your paper's "Table 1"

Every clinical paper opens with this: one row per variable, the centre and the spread side by side. Here it is, computed for all five continuous variables.

⌨️ The Stata command (you'll run this in the hands-on)
tabstat age sbp bmi crp los, ///
    stats(n mean sd min p25 median p75 max) columns(statistics)
📋 Result — computed live from the 200 patients
Variable    N   Mean    SD  Min   p25  Median    p75    Max
--------  ---  -----  ----  ---  ----  ------  -----  -----
age       200   59.2  14.7   18    51      59     70     95
sbp       200  132.7  18.2   87   120     133  146.2    176
bmi       200   27.7   5.1   15  24.3    28.1   31.4     45
crp       200    7.0   9.9  0.3   2.3     4.0    7.8  100.6
los       200    5.0   3.1    1     3       4      6     18
💡 What this means
Read across each row. For the symmetric variables (age, SBP, BMI) the mean ≈ median, so report mean (SD). For crp and los the mean sits well above the median — skewed — so report median (p25–p75) instead. The same table tells you both the numbers and which numbers to quote.
🚗 In the hands-on (Stata's auto data)
tabstat mpg price weight, stats(n mean sd median p25 p75) gives the identical layout for the cars.
🗣️ Say this
This single table is Table 1 of your paper. The trick is choosing mean-or-median per row — and the median/p25/p75 columns make that obvious.

6 · The shape of the data — histograms

A table gives numbers; a histogram gives shape. It's the single most useful plot in descriptive statistics.

⌨️ The Stata command (you'll run this in the hands-on)
histogram age, normal
📈 Graph — drawn from the data
💡 What this means
Each bar counts how many patients fall in that slice of age. The red curve is the Normal ("bell") curve with the same mean and SD. Here the bars track the bell closely and the mean (dashed) and median (dotted) almost coincide — a textbook symmetric, roughly-Normal variable. , normal overlays that reference bell for you.
🚗 In the hands-on (Stata's auto data)
histogram mpg, normal — mileage is fairly bell-shaped too.
🗣️ Say this
The histogram is the data's fingerprint. Age fills the bell evenly — symmetric, centred, no long tail.

7 · When the mean lies — skew, and the fix

Right-skew is the norm in medicine: CRP, troponin, viral load, cost, length of stay. Here's what it looks like and what to do about it.

⌨️ The Stata command (you'll run this in the hands-on)
histogram crp, normal
generate ln_crp = ln(crp)
histogram ln_crp, normal
📈 Graph — drawn from the data
💡 What this means
Left: raw CRP piles up at low values with a long tail of very sick patients to the right. The mean (7.0) is dragged into that tail, well past the median (4.0) — so the mean over-states the 'typical' patient. Right: taking the natural log pulls the tail in and the distribution becomes roughly symmetric. That's why skewed labs are often log-transformed before analysis, and reported as median (IQR).
🚗 In the hands-on (Stata's auto data)
Car price is the skewed one: histogram price, normal then gen lnprice = ln(price) and re-plot.
🗣️ Say this
Right-skew means a few extreme patients stretch the tail. Don't average it raw — report the median, and log-transform if you need to model it.
⚠️ Watch out
A positive skewness value (see the summarize output) confirms a right tail. 0 ≈ symmetric; above ~1 is clearly skewed.

8 · The box-and-whisker plot — five numbers at a glance

The boxplot is a histogram's compact cousin: it draws the five-number summary and flags outliers automatically.

⌨️ The Stata command (you'll run this in the hands-on)
graph box los
📈 Graph — drawn from the data
💡 What this means
The box spans Q1 to Q3 (the middle 50% — the IQR), the line inside is the median, and the whiskers reach to the most extreme non-outlier values. The red dots beyond the whiskers are statistical outliers (here, the long-stay patients). A box pushed to the left with a long right whisker is the boxplot's way of showing right-skew.
🚗 In the hands-on (Stata's auto data)
graph box price, or split by group with graph box mpg, over(foreign).
🗣️ Say this
Box = middle half, line = median, dots = outliers. One glance tells you centre, spread, and who the unusual patients are.

9 · Is it Normal? Q–Q plot + Shapiro–Wilk

Many later tests assume a variable is roughly Normal. Two ways to check — one visual, one a formal test.

⌨️ The Stata command (you'll run this in the hands-on)
qnorm age
swilk age
qnorm crp
swilk crp
📋 Result — computed live from the 200 patients
Variable      W  p-value  Verdict (p>0.05 = Normal)
--------  -----  -------  -------------------------
age       0.994    0.573  Normal                   
crp       0.535   <0.001  not Normal
📈 Graph — drawn from the data
💡 What this means
In a Q–Q plot the dots are your data, the red line is perfect Normality. Age hugs the line; CRP bends away at the top — its heavy tail. The Shapiro–Wilk test puts a number on it. The rule is counter-intuitive: p > 0.05 means Normal enough. Age passes (p = 0.573); CRP fails decisively, matching its skew.
🚗 In the hands-on (Stata's auto data)
qnorm mpg and swilk mpg; then try swilk price to see a skewed variable fail.
🗣️ Say this
Q–Q plot first — points on the line means Normal. Shapiro–Wilk confirms it, but remember: here a BIG p-value is the good news.
⚠️ Watch out
In big samples Shapiro–Wilk flags trivial deviations as 'significant'. Always trust the plot and the histogram over the p-value alone.

10 · Counting categories — frequencies & proportions

For categorical variables there's no mean — just how many, and what share.

⌨️ The Stata command (you'll run this in the hands-on)
tabulate ward
📋 Result — computed live from the 200 patients
ward      Freq.  Percent   Cum.
--------  -----  -------  -----
Medical      92     46.0   46.0
Surgical     72     36.0   82.0
ICU          36     18.0  100.0
Total       200    100.0
📈 Graph — drawn from the data
💡 What this means
A frequency table gives the count, the percent of the cohort, and the running cumulative percent. The bar chart is its picture. These percentages are the building blocks of risk, prevalence and proportions — and the numerators/denominators behind every rate you'll quote.
🚗 In the hands-on (Stata's auto data)
tabulate foreign (domestic vs foreign cars) or tabulate rep78.
🗣️ Say this
Categories get counted, not averaged. The percent column is what ends up in your results: '50% were medical admissions'.

11 · Describing by group — the seed of every comparison

The moment you summarise an outcome within each group, you've set up the comparison that the next lecture (t-tests, ANOVA) will test formally.

⌨️ The Stata command (you'll run this in the hands-on)
tabstat sbp, by(ward) stats(n mean sd min max)
graph bar (mean) sbp, over(ward)
📋 Result — computed live from the 200 patients
ward       N  Mean SBP    SD  Min  Max
--------  --  --------  ----  ---  ---
Medical   92     132.9  17.1   87  176
Surgical  72     133.5  20.1   87  174
ICU       36     130.7  17.2   98  173
📈 Graph — drawn from the data
💡 What this means
We split the cohort by ward and describe SBP within each. Now you can see whether ICU patients run higher than medical patients — descriptively. This is the right-hand side of every Table 1 (outcome by group), and the honest first step before any p-value: look at the group means and spreads first.
🚗 In the hands-on (Stata's auto data)
tabstat mpg, by(foreign) stats(n mean sd) — do foreign cars really get better mileage? Describe it here, test it next lecture.
🗣️ Say this
Summarise the outcome inside each group first. The difference you see here is exactly what a t-test or ANOVA will later tell you is real or not.
⚠️ Watch out
A difference in means is not yet a significant difference — that needs a test, and a test needs the assumptions we checked above. Describe first, test later.

Plain-English glossary — every term

MeanThe arithmetic average. Best for symmetric data; pulled toward the tail by skew.
MedianThe middle value (50th percentile). Half the patients lie below it. Unaffected by outliers — the honest centre for skewed data.
ModeThe most common value. Mainly useful for categories or counts.
RangeMax − min. Simple, but moves with a single extreme value.
IQR (interquartile range)p75 − p25: the width of the middle 50% of patients. Robust to outliers; the spread you pair with a median.
VarianceThe average squared distance from the mean. In squared units, so hard to read directly — mostly a stepping-stone to the SD.
SD (standard deviation)Typical distance from the mean, in the original units. ~95% of roughly-Normal data lies within mean ± 2 SD.
Percentile / quartileThe value below which a given % of patients fall. Q1=25th, Q2=median=50th, Q3=75th.
SkewnessA number for asymmetry. 0 ≈ symmetric; positive = long right tail (the medical norm); above ~1 is clearly skewed.
Normal distributionThe symmetric bell curve. Many tests assume it; check with a histogram, Q–Q plot and Shapiro–Wilk.
Q–Q plotPlots your data's quantiles against a perfect Normal. On the straight line = Normal; bending away = skewed or heavy-tailed.
Shapiro–Wilk (swilk)A formal test of Normality. p > 0.05 = Normal enough. (Counter-intuitively, a BIG p-value is the good result.)
Frequency tableCounts and percentages for a categorical variable — the basis of proportions, prevalence and rates.
